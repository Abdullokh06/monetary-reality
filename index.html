Banks are not intermadiaries.

Standart macroeconomic models treat money as a circulating object between agents - household,firms and the state - while excluding banks as balance-sheet creators.

Banks appear only as intermediaries - transferring existing money from savers to borrowers.

In reality, banks do something fundametally different. They create money when they issue loans.

If banks are excluded from the model, the mechanism of money creation disappears.

Why balance sheets matter?

Economic models usually focus on flows: income, spending, investment. Balance sheets, however, record stocks: assets, liabilities and equity. Money is soehow a stock, not a flow. If balance sheets are ignored, money itself becomes invisible.

Flows vs stocks.

Macroeconomic textbooks describe the economy as a circulation of money. But circulation diagrams hide the institutiona structure that creates money. Balance sheets make this structure explicit.

Money is an accounting fact

Money is not a theoretical construct. It is an accounting entry recorded on balance sheets. Every unit of money exists as a liability of the banking system. This is not a hypothesis - it is a fact documented by central banks.

Central bank balance sheets

Central banks publish detailed balance sheets. These documents show how money enters the economy, how it is transformed through the banking system and how it is ultimately extinguished.

This project relies exclusively on publicly available data from central banks and international institutions. No assumptions about behavior are required. The structure of money is visible in the accountins themselves.

What models assume and what they ommit

Most macroeconomic models do not deny exostence of banks. They simply abstract from them Bnaks are reduced to passive intermediaries and the act of money creation is excluded by assumption.

As a result, money appaers as an exogenous variable, not as an outcome of institutional processes. Crises then appear as shocks, rather than as consequences of balance sheet dynamics

Absence as an assumption

What is absent from a model is often more important than what is explicity included. When banks are excluded, credit-driven expansions and contractions cannot appear endogenously.

If banks are omitted, crises become mysterious

In standart macroeconomic models, financial crises appear as external shock. Something unexpected happens and the system deviates from equilibrium.

But if banks create money through credit, then expansions and contractions of balance sheets are not shocks. They are the normal dynamics of the system.

Crises are not anomalies

When credit grows faster than incomes, balance sheets stretch. When repayment dominates new lending, money disappears. In such a system, instability is not an exception - it is a structual outcome.
